Iconic subway seen to outlast pandemic's lasting effects

Trains shunned, but not abandoned, depict resilience of New York's lifeblood


August 12, 2022*

When Noelle Morcos finally decided to work fully remote for Meta starting in August, she weighed the costs and benefits of doing so: on one hand, she'll lose access to office perks and the chance to bond with her teammates in person. On the other though, she no longer needed to rush to the office, could walk her dog in between virtual meetings, and most especially no longer had to ride the subway.
Employees like Morcos have shunned the subway recently, whether because they simply have no place to go and working from home, or done so intentionally over anxieties triggered by rising criminality (which also contributed to her decision). Regardless of reason, the impact on the train system was palpable with ridership barely around 60% of their levels in 2019 just before the health crisis shut down the city.
Yet even as riders avoided the subway, they never completely abandoned it. Morcos unhesitatingly boards the subway on weekends, relying on its vast network to meet friends for dinners and run errands on her free day. She said she wouldn't have it any other way. "There's no better way to get to your destination fast and the subway is also less crowded during weekends so it's very convenient," she said.

On average, the subway shuttles over 3.1 million passengers on a weekday, and nearly 2 million during weekends, according to data from the Metropolitan Transit Authority (MTA). The numbers pale in comparison with their pre-pandemic levels, but, as Yonah Freemark, senior research associate at D.C.-based Urban Institute puts it, "that's still a lot of people" and crowding in coaches is still very much common during peak hours these days.
That familiar scenario may speak of many things that have been ignored on the aging infrastructure — inadequate federal and state aid plus huge budget deficits set to reach $4.8 billion this year are just some of the often-cited reasons for underinvestment. But when looked using the lens of how people have depended on the subway not just to reach their destinations, but also to have fun, or in some cases earn money, the dire image changes and reflects instead how the subway has simply become the lifeblood of the city.
Hence despite the grim financial prospects, analysts are one in saying that the subway is here to stay. Changes likely abound though, and it already started with a more subdued ridership. That said, the subway, one of the largest in the world, remains the best way to go around the city and will continue to be so without a more efficient alternative.
"The subway plays an incredibly important role in just assuring basic daily mobility for the majority of people," Freemark said.
By no means that the subway is a natural monopoly, however. On the ground, people ride cars and buses. Cabs are hailed, and Uber rides are booked. But these alternatives get beaten by the subway for punctuality. Sure, the subway also gets delayed— MTA data showed that in May, passengers waited an additional 82 seconds than usual during peak hours on weekdays— but unless maintenance work is ongoing or, God forbid, an accident happens, a commuter can easily calculate how fast (or slow) they are to reach their destination. That is way much better than getting stuck on the world's worst traffic congestion.
New York is so dense as a city that there's no longer space to build new roads, highways or even parking lots to accommodate more vehicles, Freemark said. "That's what makes the subway a fundamental part of the city," he said.

This reliability, of course, is coupled with affordability. Hanna Nicole Daanoy, 24, takes the F- or E-train from Queens to W 4th Street to report to work daily. A restaurant manager at L' Artusi in West Village, she sends money back to her family in the Philippines as monthly allowance which means she must always follow a strict budget.
While typically she goes straight from work to home using the train, going out late with friends during weekends sometimes force her to take an Uber ride and go off-budget. "I'm afraid to take the subway alone at night because of what is happening. Sometimes I just not go out to save money," Daanoy said, referring to the spate of crimes in the subway recently.
Using Uber's own calculator, an UberX home would cost Daanoy roughly $20 at any given time, nearly 10 times she would spend by riding the train home. "I can't afford taking a cab or Uber everyday. I won't be able to eat anymore," Daanoy said in jest.
In a time when prices of several items from potatoes to real estate are soaring, MTA fares have been steady for nearly three years now. The management was supposed to implement a 4% fare hike in 2021 but decided against it to ease the burden of customers reeling from the pandemic.
It remains to be seen whether the MTA would finally push through with the fare increase later this year, but when the postponement was announced a year ago, Patrick Foye, former MTA chair and chief executive, cited the need to attract back riders, who, theoretically, would be deterred by a larger fare.
Things got a little bit better since then. Average weekday ridership rose to 57% of their 2019 levels as of the third week of July, up from just about 49% same period a year ago, MTA data showed. Ridership is finally rising from the depths of pandemic, and higher gas prices that are likely to deter driving may prove to be a further boost, Thomas Abdallah, deputy vice president at the MTA, said.
But the subway continues to be patronized doesn't mean the challenges it faces are not as daunting. For instance, while already increasing, ridership remains far way low than what is considered normal in pre-pandemic times. The MTA, in October, projected a “new normal” for ridership where it stays above 1.5 billion annually, compared with about 1.7 billion before 2020. That presents a big challenge for the agency, which relies on fare revenues for 28% of its annual operating budget.
Remote working, which is likely to become entrenched even with Covid-19 under control, is to blame for the bleak outlook, Abdallah said. Our analysis showed that ridership has a significant direct relationship with office occupancy. To put simply, when people started working from home, there is less reason for them to travel and subway passengers consequently nose-dived.

The MTA embarked on several efforts to attract back its old patrons, including adding more train services, offering discounts, and a fare-cap mechanism. Under the latter, riders using the OMNY fare system get unlimited free rides for the rest of the week after their 12th consecutive ride. The program's impact has not been officially assessed, but Abdallah said its effect on ridership would have been limited at best.
"Most people take the train 10 times a week, going to the office and back, and that's during normal times," he said, indicating that there may only be a few people availing of the fare-cap promo.
Safety risks, or at least the perception of it, is another issue. News of people getting shot and pushed over the tracks in the last few months have scared riders away. Crime's effect on total ridership has not been formally studied, but Danny Pearlstein, policy communications director at Riders Alliance, a commuter's group, was sure it at least has some bearing on personal decisions to ride the subway or not.
While such reservations are valid, Pearlstein stressed that by and large, the subway remains safe to ride. His assessment is supported by official data: crime rates have been low despite the increase in crime incidents and there has been less than one crime committed per 100,000 people on subway stations.
“Crime isn't nearly so dramatic as the focus on it would suggest,” Pearlstein said.
The city responded to crimes by increasing police presence in stations. Passengers however have started taking precautions themselves. Morcos now avoids standing at the platform's edge when waiting for the next train ride. Onboard, she listens to podcasts with only a piece of an Airpod on, while the other ear stays alert of the surroundings.
Still, some others like Sung Hee Joon, 24, chose the drastic approach of avoiding the subway at all costs. "It's just not worth the anxiety," she said.
Despite the challenges, Freemark is optimistic that ridership finally bottomed out and the uptrend will be sustained. Against other metrics, subway ridership has lagged behind retail sales, job generation, and Broadway ticket sales when it comes to pandemic recovery. In-person classes have long resumed, and tourists are flying back to the Big Apple, yet none of them successfully ramped up ridership to a level the MTA would want it to be.
Ultimately, the jury is still out as to the type of recovery the subway is headed to and observers have varying thoughts about it. Abdallah and Pearlstein, for instance, believe the subway management should stop chasing a 100% recovery and adjust to a "new normal."
For Abdallah, that means devising an operational plan for the subway around current ridership, which may include changes in the number of and time trains get deployed. "Maybe we have less people in the morning than we normally did. But we have more people at noon or at night. I don't know the exact answer, but we need to see how this plays out," he explained.
"I think this it," he said, referring to ridership. "We have to figure out how we can proceed from here."
Robert Paaswell, engineering professor at City University of New York, however disagrees. "People want to be in crowds, want to be with their office mates or go to the restaurant or bars," he said.
"People are in New York because they want to experience New York and the best way to do that is to ride the subway," Paaswell said. Prinz Magtulis
