Iconic subway seen to outlast pandemic's lasting effects

Trains shunned, but not abandoned, depict reliance on New York's lifeblood


August 12, 2022

When Noelle Morcos finally decided to work fully remote for Meta starting in August, she weighed the costs and benefits of doing so: on one hand, she'll lose access to office perks and the chance to bond with her teammates in person. On the other, she no longer needed to rush to the office, could walk her dog in between virtual meetings, and most especially be able to avoid the subway.
Employees like Morcos have minimized riding the subway recently, whether because they work remotely, or fear of rising crime (which also contributed to her decision). Regardless of reason, the impact on the train system was palpable and enduring— over a year since mobility restrictions were lifted, ridership hovers just about 60% of their pre-pandemic level in 2019.
Yet even as riders avoided the subway, they never completely abandoned it. Morcos unhesitatingly boards the train on weekends, relying on its vast network to meet friends and run errands. "There's no better way to get to your destination fast and the subway is also less crowded during weekends so it's very convenient," she said.
On average, the subway shuttles over 3.1 million passengers on a weekday, and nearly 2 million during weekends, according to data from the Metropolitan Transit Authority (MTA). The numbers pale in comparison with their pre-pandemic levels, but, as Yonah Freemark, senior research associate at Washington, D.C.-based Urban Institute puts it, "that's still a lot of people" and crowding in coaches is still very much common during peak hours these days.
That familiar scenario may speak of the many things ignored of the aging infrastructure — inadequate federal and state aid plus huge budget deficits set to reach $4.8 billion this year meant underinvestment. But when examined using the lens of how New Yorkers depended on the subway for most of their lives, the dire image instead represents an infrastructure that has become the lifeblood of the city.
Hence despite grim financial prospects, analysts are one in saying that the subway is here to stay. Changes likely abound though, and many argue they already started with decreased ridership. The continued patronage however reflects one thing: the subway, one of the largest in the world, remains the best way to go around the city and will continue to be so without a more efficient alternative.
"The subway plays an incredibly important role in just assuring basic daily mobility for the majority of people," Freemark said.
New York's reliance on the subway is not because of inadequate transport options. Indeed, on the ground, buses ply the roads, cabs are hailed, and Uber rides are booked. Nearly half of the city's households also own a car. But these alternatives, while always available, get beaten by the subway for efficiency and punctuality. Sure, the subway also gets delayed— MTA data showed that in May, passengers waited an additional 82 seconds than usual during peak hours on weekdays— but unless maintenance work is ongoing or, God forbid, an accident happens, commuters can easily estimate how long their trip will be.
New York traffic is the worst among key cities in the U.S., and the city is so dense that there's no space to build new roads, highways or even parking lots to accommodate more vehicles, Freemark said. "That's what makes the subway a fundamental part of the city," he said.

The subway's reliability is coupled with fare affordability. Hanna Nicole Daanoy, 24, takes the F- or E-train from Queens to W 4th Street to report to work daily. A restaurant manager at L' Artusi in West Village, she sends money to her family in the Philippines as monthly allowance which means she follows a strict budget that does not include an Uber ride.
Using Uber's own fare calculator, an Uber X ride going home would cost Daanoy roughly $20 at the minimum, nearly tenfold the subway fare. "I'm afraid to take the subway alone at night because of what is happening. Sometimes I just not go out to save money," Daanoy said, citing the spate of crimes for avoiding the subway at night.
"I can't afford to take a cab or Uber every time. I won't be able to eat anymore," she said in jest.
While prices of several items from potatoes to real estate are soaring with inflation, MTA fares have been steady for nearly three years now. The management was supposed to hike fares by 4% in 2021 but decided against it to ease the burden of customers reeling from the pandemic.
It remains to be seen whether the fare increase would push through this year, but when the initial delay was announced, Patrick Foye, former MTA chair and chief executive, also cited the need to attract back riders, who, theoretically, would have been deterred by higher fares.
New normal for MTA
Since then, ridership improved a bit. Data showed average weekday passengers rose to 57% of their 2019 level on the third week of July, up from just about 49% same period a year ago. Thomas Abdallah, deputy vice president and chief environmental engineer at the MTA, said with gas prices still elevated, car owners may "think twice" of paying more at the pump, ride the subway instead, and push up ridership further.
But returning to pre-pandemic level may no longer be possible. In October, the MTA projected a "new normal" of about 1.5 billion customers annually by 2024, down from around 1.7 billion before 2020, citing a "change on behavior" among commuters for the projected decline. Abdallah said remote work is to blame as it becomes entrenched in the society even when the pandemic is over.
Our analysis showed that office occupancy tends to significantly influence subway ridership. That means that when people started not reporting to office, they lost a prime reason to take the subway, consequently resulting into low passenger volume.

The MTA embarked on several efforts to attract its old patrons back, including adding more train services, offering discounts, and a fare-cap mechanism. Under the latter, commuters using the OMNY fare system get unlimited free rides for the rest of the week after their 12th consecutive ride. The program's effectiveness has not been officially assessed, but Abdallah said the impact on ridership would have been limited at best.
"Most people take the train 10 times a week, going to the office and back, and that's during normal times," he said, indicating that there may only be a few people availing of the promo.
Still safe
Safety is another issue. The perception that riding the subway is unsafe has grown because of several stories of people getting shot and pushed over the tracks. This, in turn, has scared off both old and new riders.
Mio Tomita arrived in New York in June for a summer program and has been taking the subway from 96th Street station to Columbia. But come August when her mom and daughter join her in the city, she said there'll be no more subway trips for her. "To be honest, a lot of my colleagues here tell me not to take the subway," said Tomita, who works as a reporter for Nikkei Asia in Japan.
"If it's just me, it's okay. But my mom and daughter see the news from here and getting on the subway make them scared," Tomita said.
While concerns about criminality are valid, Danny Pearlstein, policy communications director at Riders Alliance, a commuter's group, stressed that the subway remains remarkably safe. "Crime isn't nearly so dramatic as the focus on it would suggest," he said.
Government data support this. Crime rates are low despite a jump in incidents over the past two years, and there has been less than one crime committed per 100,000 people on subway stations.
In short, Pearlstein said passengers ride the subway because they still trust arriving to their destination "without incident." The city has also been responsive to safety concerns, increasing police presence in stations during peak hours. Mayor Eric Adams also pledged to augment response teams and open more "safe haven" centers to assist riders.
For the most part however, Freemark said commuters choose to ride the subway because the benefits of convenience and getting to destination on time still outweigh the risks. And this shows even among people who have grown averse of the subway: Morcos said she doesn't foresee ditching the subway anytime soon. Daanoy said it's just impossible to do it, while Tomito, a tourist, said it remains an option for her when visiting.
Attracting passengers lost back to the system is good, but Abdallah said it also may be time to shift focus to the ones left. Planning for a "new normal", he said, involves devising a plan that involves the efficient use of subway resources. “I don't know the exact answer,” he said, but added that assessing whether current peak and non-peak hours are still accurate, given remote work, is a good starting point.
Pearlstein, meanwhile, is advocating for more state and federal aid to offset losses from fare revenues. The latter account for 28% of MTA's annual operating budget so if ridership, as MTA projects, will now be lower than pre-pandemic normal, money used to sustain operations should come from somewhere else. The last federal aid was given last year as part of Covid-19 response, but transport advocates are calling for more.
Against other metrics, subway ridership has lagged behind retail sales, job generation, and Broadway ticket sales when it comes to pandemic recovery. In-person classes have long resumed, and tourists are flying back to the Big Apple, yet none of them successfully ramped up ridership to a level the MTA would want it to be.
While the jury is still out as to how the subway's recovery will look like, one thing for sure is it will never be left deserted. "I don't think it's going to decline further," Freemark said, pertaining to ridership. And if Robert Paaswell, engineering professor at City University of New York, is to be believed, a full rebound may even be possible.
"People want to be in crowds, want to be with their office mates or go to the restaurant or bars," he said.
"People are in New York because they want to experience New York and the best way to do that is to ride the subway," Paaswell said. Prinz Magtulis
